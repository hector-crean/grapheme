Diagnosis

Diagnosis of MDD

Based on the DSM-5, in order to be diagnosed, a patient must have experienced 5 or more of the following symptoms during a 2-week period. At least one of the symptoms should be either depressed mood or anhedonia:

Depressed mood
Anhedonia
Change in weight or appetite
Disturbance in sleep patterns
Psychomotor agitation or slowing
Loss of energy or fatigue
Feelings of worthlessness or guilt
Impaired ability to think/concentrate
Thoughts of suicide or death

Benefits of Early Diagnosis

Several studies have highlighted the importance of early detection and intervention in MDD and its relationship to improving outcomes.

Examples of the benefits of early diagnosis include

  1. Improvement in employee productivity and decreased absenteeism in the workplace1
  2. Higher remission rates1
  3. Improvements in social function1
  4. Less depression-related disability post-pharmacotherapy2
  5. Faster response to antidepressant treatments2

Consequences of delayed treatment

It has been reported that delayed treatment users can be distinguished from early treatment users by two need for care factors:

  1. Reporting less than two weeks of disability days3
  2. Reporting none/mild symptoms3

Misdiagnosis and getting appropriate treatment

Several studies have highlighted the importance of early detection and intervention in MDD and its relationship to improving outcomes.

Examples of the benefits of early diagnosis include

  1. Treatment of mood disorders can be challenging as symptoms overlap among the different disorders1.
  2. Misdiagnosing another disorder as depression could lead to delays in treatment for other disorders or inappropriate, potentially harmful interventions3.
  3. The diagnostic classification of mood disorders is a dynamic process that is problematic for clinicians and researchers alike2.

Bipolar disorder

  • The first episode of mood disturbance in bipolar disorder is often depression rather than mania4.
  • Hence, most patients with bipolar illness seek treatment only for depressive symptoms, making the correct diagnosis difficult4.
  • An incorrect diagnosis of unipolar depression carries the risk of inappropriate treatment with antidepressants, which can result in manic episodes and trigger rapid cycling4.

Chronic fatigue syndrome

  • Fatigue is a symptom in many diseases such as depression, anxiety, seasonal affective disorder.
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS) is undiagnosed in 80% of cases and is often misdiagnosed as depression6.
  • In clinical practice, CFS presentations range from complicated cases associated with a psychotic state resulting in multiple murders in one case report to noncomplicated presentations with multiple psychiatric disorders, primarily depression6.
  • It is very important to understand the distinctive features between chronic fatigue and depressive disorder when evaluating a patient with a main complaint of fatigue because CFS is a diagnosis of exclusion6.

Pseudobulbar affect (PBA)

  • Pseudobulbar affect (PBA) is a neuropsychiatric condition associated with various neurological conditions, including strokes7.
  • PBA affects approximately 1 in 5 stroke survivors7.
  • It remains under-recognized and is often misdiagnosed with psychiatric conditions like depression and bipolar disorder7.
  • If diagnosed, PBA could be managed with appropriate medication7.
  • However, clinical depression can happen in the presence of PBA if left unmanaged7.

Hypothyroidism

  • The deficiency of thyroid hormones in the CNS can cause fatigue, weight gain and lack of energy, all of which are symptoms of clinical depression8.
  • Psychiatric symptoms of hypothyroidism are often primary and prominent and include impaired memory, psychomotor slowing and reduced attention span, all of which are symptoms of MDD8.
  • Depression occurs in 69% of people diagnosed with hypothyroidism making it difficult to distinguish between the two9.
