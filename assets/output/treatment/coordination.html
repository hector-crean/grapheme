Coordination of Care in Bipolar Disorder

Only 40% of patients with mood disorders receive appropriate treatment

Patients are typically only seen and treated in primary care from the lack of access to mental health services

Outpatient treatment for patients with BD has 3 major goals:

  1. Monitor and support the medication
  2. Develop and maintain the therapeutic alliance
  3. Provide education to the patient and their family

The impact of providing care for someone with bipolar disorder is significant, requiring more hours of caregiving and incurring increased direct healthcare costs:

Collaborative Care in Bipolar disorder

"...The collaborative care model, in which patients are active managers of their illness within a supportive social environment is a beneficial approach of individuals with bipolar disorder."

Collaborative care integrates both physical and behavioral health servicesand involves the collaboration between:

  • A care manager driving the mental health treatment in primary care;
  • A primary care physician;
  • A consulting psychiatrist in regular review with care manager

Collaborative effort is beneficial in helping individuals with bipolar disorder such as helping to adhere to treatment

Collaborative care has the potential to reduce the number of weeks an individual is in manic episode

In a case study, collaborative care reduced bipolar symptoms and improved functioning in an individual with bipolar disorder

In a randomized controlled trial of collaborative care vs. usual care, patients with both major depression disorder and bipolar disorder in a collaborative chronic care model showed improved scores on the nine-item Patient Health Questionnaire (PHQ-9; A) and mental health-related quality of life (SF-12 MCS; B)assessing depressive symptoms and mental health components, respectively

Whilst collaborative care models have shown beneficial effects, however research is limited
