Adherence to treatment of Bipolar Disorder

"Psychotropic non-adherence in those with BD contributes to a gap in medication efficacy and effectiveness."

Bipolar disorder is a cyclical disorder where the occurrence of mania or hypomania either alternate or occur with the depressive episodes.

Approximately 20-60% rate of non-adherence to medication is estimated.

40-50% of bipolar disorder patients are thought to not take medication regularly.

Up to 40% of patients with mania are either partially or completely nonadherent with their prescribed medications.

Rate of 40.2% 'in the past week' and 42.8% 'in the past month' of non-adherence was self-reported in one study.

Associated with increased risk of:

  • Earlier onset.
  • Greater need for health-care services.
  • Increased hospital admissions.
  • Mental health expenditures.
  • Poor quality of life.
  • Rapid cycling.
  • Comorbidity with anxiety, alcohol use disorder, obesity, obsessive compulsive disorder (OCD)
  • Relapse.
  • Reoccurrence of acute manic episodes.
  • Suicide.

Reasons for non-adherence1:

Patient-level

  • Younger age
  • Unmarried marital status
  • Negative attitude toward treatment
  • Stigma
  • Comorbidities
  • Severe psychotic symptoms
  • Cognitive deficits

External

  • Medication type
  • Side-effects of medication
  • Poor relationship with clinician
  • Poor access to care

Implications on the Bipolar brain:

Decreased leftvolume is observed in untreated patients compared to those treated with a mood stabilizer, lithium, and healthy participants.

volumes have been shown to be smaller in patients who have had no or limited treatment with lithium compared to healthy participantsin fact, 14% larger hippocampal volume is reported in lithium-treated patients compared to untreated patients.

Total grey matter volume has been shown to be increased in patients treated with lithium, compared to untreated patients and healthy participants; over a 4 week period, grey matter volume was reported to be increased by 3% in Bipolar patients.

Gaps in treatment of Bipolar Disorder

"[Bipolar disorder] BD is often unrecognized or misdiagnosed. As a consequence, BD has a long untreated period."

Bipolar disorder is episodic, where approximately 50% of patient’s first episode is depressive proving diagnosis difficult.

Delay to first time treatment is associated with higher levels of depression, and higher number of episodes.

One study reported an average of 9.6 years of untreated Bipolar disorder in patients with Bipolar I and II, whilst a gap of 3 years was observed between the first mood episode and treatment.

During the 2 years following treatment, 43.5% patients underwent first suicide attempt and 94% patients were hospitalized.

It is estimated to take 5-10 years for treatment commencement from onset of illness to diagnosis.

Delays in treatment is associated with increased risk of rapid cyclingand, excessive cannabis useand suicide.

Implications on the Bipolar brain:

Decreased leftvolume is observed in untreated patients compared to those treated with a mood stabilizer, lithium, and healthy participants.

volumes have been shown to be smaller in patients who have had no treatment with lithium compared to healthy participants.
