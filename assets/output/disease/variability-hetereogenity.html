Age variability

  • Longer episodes and shorter breaks between episodes in older people with BD.
  • Younger people have higher intrasubject variability in response time, relating to deficits in maintaining concentration and arousal.
  • Affrontive symptoms are associated more strongly with older patients.

Cognitive and Functional Variably

  • It has been shown that patients with bipolar disorder exhibit differences in cognitive abilities.
  • Cognitive abnormalities can involve attention, memory and executive functioning, possibly due to brain abnormalities seen in some patients.
  • Functional impairment has been shown to vary depending on mood symptoms experienced by patients with bipolar disorder.

Gender variability

  • Men with BD 1 more likely to have a manic episode at the beginning of the disorder, whereas women are more likely to have depressive episodes at the early stages of the disorder.

Comorbidity Variability

  • Substance abuse and dependence is more common in men than women with BD.
  • Antisocial behaviors are more likely in men than women with BD.
  • Women are more likely to experience changes in appetitesand develop eating disorders.
  • Early onset and rapid cycling is associated with higher incidence of anxiety.
  • Changes in the autonomic nervous system and heart rate.
  • Comorbid conditions are related to age of onset and familial loading.
  • Remitting episodic course is more likely to respond to Lithium and not develop comorbidities.

Treatment Variability in Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar disorder is complex, comprises of subtypes that responds differently to different treatments ,, with an estimated third of patients not responding to treatment at all. Approximately 44% of people with bipolar disorder experience relapse within in 1 year andincreasing to 73% in 5 years.

Lithium

  • 49% of patients respond witha reduction manic episodes by up to 38% and depressive relapse episodes by up to 28%
  • Side effects include reduced renal function and hyperparathyroidism.
  • 20-40% of BD suffers do not respond to lithium treatment.
  • Lithium has been shown to reduce suicide by up to 50%.

First Generation
Antipsychotics

  • Average 47% response rate.
  • Long-term use not advised due to negative side effects.

Second Generation
Antipsychotics

  • Average 28% of patients were stable for over a year.
  • However second generation antipsychotics have been linked to higher suicidal ideation.

Anticonvulsants

  • There are a limited number of anticonvulsants that are effective mood stabilizers.
  • Average 48% response ratewith 37-48% failure rate in comparison to lithium.

Antidepressants

  • Long term use is not recommendedas their benefits are often minorand some patients experience increased numbers of manic episodes, rapid cyclingand dysphoriawith antidepressants
  • Risk of spontaneous mania in some patients without antidepressants has been shown to be 14%.

Cognitive behavior Therapy (CBT)

  • Commonly used to treat depression and anxiety disorders.
  • CBT, in combination with medication and psychological support, has been shown to reduce manic and depressive relapses, the length of episodes, hospital admissionsand improve global functioningbut benefits may only be short termand depend on disease severity.

Family-Focused Therapy (FFT)

  • Involves educating the patient and family.
  • FFT has shown improvement to adherence to medications through education.
  • FFT is associated with improved recovery times from depressive symptoms,but has been shown to have little effect on manic symptoms.
  • Multiple family sessions are required which are not always possible.

Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy (IPSRT)

  • Aims to resolve any interpersonal problems that occurred during the persons most recent episode through regulating improve day and night-time routines.
  • IPSRT has been shown to increase time between episodes if treatment was given during the acute phase.

Electroconductive Therapy (ECT)

  • There is contradictory evidence that ECT improves the rates of hospital admissions.
  • More commonly a second-line treatment for severe bipolar disorder.
  • Approximately 30% of patients with bipolar type 1 respond to ECT treatment.
  • Long times between treatments have been shown to be less effective and maintenance treatment requirements are varied.
  • Negative responses such as cognitive side effects and cardiac problems can occur.

Omega-3 Fatty acids

  • Have been shown to increase remission times through mild improvements of depressive symptoms in some patients with bipolar disorder, but no effect on manic symptoms.
  • Results are heavily dependent on the type of fatty acid used, the dose and if additional medications were taken.
