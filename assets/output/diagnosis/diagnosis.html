Diagnosis

Diagnosis of Bipolar Disorder

Based on the DSM-5, to be diagnosed with bipolar disorder, a person must have experienced at least one episode of mania or hypomania, plus a major depressive episode.

Mania

A distinct period of 1 week or more (less if hospitalization is required) during which patients experience abnormally and persistently raised, expansive, or irritable mood. The mood disturbance and increased energy or activity must be accompanied by ≥3 additional symptoms listed below. If the mood is irritable (rather than elevated or expansive), at least 4 of the below symptoms must be present.

Hypomania

Distinct period of abnormally and persistently elevated, expansive, or irritable mood and abnormally and persistently increased activity or energy, lasting ≥4 days, not sufficiently severe to cause pronounced impairment in social or occupational functioning or to necessitate hospitalization.

Major depressive episode

A period of 2 weeks with either depressed mood or anhedonia. The individual must also experience ≥4 additional symptoms listed below.

Manic/hypomanic symptoms from the DSM-5 criteria

Inflated self-esteem or grandiosity
Decreased need for sleep
Increased talkativeness
Racing thoughts
Distracted easily
Engaging in activities that hold the potential for painful consequences e.g. unrestrained shopping sprees.

Depression symptoms from the DSM-5 criteria

Depressed mood
Anhedonia
Change in weight or appetite
Disturbance in sleep patterns
Psychomotor agitation or slowing
Loss of energy or fatigue
Feelings of worthlessness or guilt
Impaired ability to think/concentrate
Thoughts of suicide or death

Early diagnosis

Benefits of early diagnosis1

  • Starting treatment during the first 10 years of the illness decreases the number of recurrences a patient experiences by half.
  • Patients respond faster to treatment the earlier it is initiated in the course of the disease.
  • Patients are less likely to receive harmful treatments than if their condition is misdiagnosed.
  • Early treatment with lithium can provide neuroprotective qualities that help the patients overall cognitive function.
  • Patients diagnosed and treated early have better social functioning which can lead to better overall quality of life.

Consequences of delayed diagnosis

One of the reasons for the unfavourable outcome of BD is that diagnosis is often delayed.

  • People with undiagnosed BD have more frequent, longer and more severe episodes.
  • Untreated BD patients are more likely to miss work and have reduced productivity.
  • Treatment with antidepressants after an MDD misdiagnosis can trigger manic or hypomanic episodes. In one study a misdiagnosis was seen in over 50% of patients, many of which were prescribed antidepressants.
  • Healthcare costs can increase dramatically for BD patients that are undiagnosed due to more frequent hospitalizations.
  • The risk of suicide attempts increases in BD patients that have not yet been properly diagnosed.
  • Long duration of untreated illness is associated with greater morbidity and a lower response to mood stabilizer treatment.

Misdiagnosis

  • BD patients are often misdiagnosed on initial presentation, most commonly with major depressive disorder (MDD).
  • The misdiagnosis rate in BD patients can reach up to 69%.
  • 35% of BD patients were symptomatic for more than 10 years before a correct diagnosis was made.
  • Patients may receive >3 misdiagnoses and see 4 physicians before a correct diagnosis is made

Major contributors to misdiagnosis include2:

  1. Incomplete history
  2. Lack of patient insight
  3. Presence of comorbidities
  4. Fear of stigma from both the patient and the HCP
  5. Lag between episodes – patients may experience depressive episodes without any mania for up to 5 years
  6. Hypomanic episode may not be memorable or go unnoticed

Consequences of misdiagnosis2:

  1. Greater number of recurrences
  2. Longer episodes
  3. Iatrogenic injury
  4. Ineffective treatment due to misdiagnosis can worsen outcome either by inducing manic or mixed episodes or increasing mood cycling

Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) vs Bipolar Disorder (BD)3

Reasons for misdiagnosis:

  • MDD is more common than BD, and because BD lacks pathognomonic features it is often incorrectly diagnosed as MDD.
  • Depression is often the presenting mood state in BD, studies have shown that patients with BD were depressed for almost 30-50% of the evaluation period making an MDD diagnosis an easy assumption to make.

Clinical characteristics that may differentiate MDD from BD:

Clinical Characteristics Typically occurs before the age of 25 Typically occurs after the age of 25
Disease course Episodes may be abrupt, often periodical or seasonal Episodes may be preceded by periods of gradually worsening symptoms
Manic episodes Treatment-emergent mania/hypomania No history of mania or hypomania
Symptoms more common Hypersomnia, psychomotor agitation, fewer physical complaints, hyperphagia and leaden paralysis Anxiety, appetite disturbances, physical complaints, initial insomnia and weight loss

Schizophrenia vs Bipolar Disorder (BD)4

Reasons for misdiagnosis:

  • Schizophrenia and BD share symptoms such as psychosis, disorganised speech, behavior problems, decreased social/occupational functioning and declining cognition
  • Previous DSM criteria may have contributed to common misdiagnosis with schizophrenia, several studies from the 1970’s and 1980’s found that nearly 50% of individuals who met the DMS-III criteria for mania had previously been diagnosed with schizophrenia. Newer DSM criteria acknowledge psychosis as a symptom of both disorders has reduced misdiagnosis.

Clinical characteristics that may differentiate Schizophrenia from BD:

  4. Grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior
  5. Diminished emotional expression or avolition

Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD) vs Bipolar Disorder (BD)4

Reasons for misdiagnosis:

  • BPD and BD share symptoms such as affective instability and impulsivity, high neuroticism and low conscientiousness
  • 12-23% of patients with BD II meet the criteria for BPD
  • Frequent comorbidity between BPD and mood disorders such as BD ranges from 35-51.5%

Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) vs Bipolar Disorder (BD)

Reasons for misdiagnosis:

  • Many symptoms are non-discriminatory between the two disorders including hyperactivity, impulsivity, short attention span, anxiety, OCD and poor frustration tolerance.
  • ADHD is more common that BD, therefore HCP's are more likely to recognise and diagnose ADHD over BD.
  • Comorbidity levels are high; 60-70% of BD patients also have ADHD and 20% of ADHD patients also have BD.
  • BD does exist in children however it is commonly misdiagnosed as ADHD as it typically manifests at a younger age then BD.

Differences between the two disorders:

  • The key difference between the two is that ADHD symptoms are contextual and situational whereas BD symptoms are triggered by external factors.
  • One of the biggest differences between the two is that BD primarily affects mood whereas ADHD primarily affects behavior and attention.
  • BD patients cycle through different episodes whereas ADHD patients experience chronic symptoms without cycles.
