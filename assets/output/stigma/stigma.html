Stigma

Researchers have defined stigma in terms of 5 interrelated components:

  • Labelling
  • Stereotyping
  • Status loss
  • Separation
  • Discrimination

These components are experienced by patients in almost all aspects of life including work, family life and marital life.

Stigma in bipolar disorder patients

  • Research has mainly focused on the stigma surrounding schizophrenia and MDD however evidence that BD patients are stigmatised in society has emerged and highlighted that these patients perceive themselves as being stigmatised.
  • Awareness of the stigma towards them leads to lower quality of life, lower self-esteem and decreased work and social functioning.
  • Stigma may have a significant negative impact on the potential of complete recovery of the BD patients social functioning.
  • BD patients concerned about stigma may adapt their social behavior to avoid exposure to rejection or discrimination. One study showed that 46% of BD patients had a tendency to try and hide their illness due to fear of stigma.
  • Criticism from within the family is frequently connected with low self–esteem and high levels of self-stigmatisation.
  • Factors such as older age of onset, unmarried status, male sex, low socio-economic level, lack of social supports and number and length of previous episodes have all been associated with stigma.
  • Stigma has a high impact on an individuals decision to seek treatment and can lead to delaying or avoiding it altogether. Likewise, BD patients that might benefit from mental health services will avoid them all together or not participate fully due to fear of stigmatization.

Stigma can manifest in multiple forms

Public Stigma

  • Negative attitudes, beliefs or behaviours of the general public towards those with mental illness[3].

Self stigma

  • Self internalization and application of negative stereotypes, which can lead to lower self-esteem and self-efficacy[3][4]

Stigma in the healthcare system and amongst HCPs

  • Lack of knowledge and understanding can drive subconscious stigma on the side of the HCP5.
  • Beliefs and attitude of HCPs can influence their decisions over treatment routes6.
  • Lack of skills and training of HCPs can lead to patient distancing and feelings of anxiety amongst HCPs, leading to less effective treatment and poorer outcomes[5].

Structural stigma

  • Includes culture and policies that limit the opportunities, resources, and welfare of people with mental illness (Conley & Baum, 2022)
  • Amplifies other types of stigma and worsens health disparities
